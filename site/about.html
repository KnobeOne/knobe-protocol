About KNOBE

Knowledge travels. The knowing that produced it often does not.

KNOBE is an attempt to make that loss visible and, where possible, prevent it. A KNOBE is a plain-text knowledge object that carries declared source context, transformation history, use conditions, fidelity limits, accessibility lineage, and a local verification seal.

KNOBE does not ask the receiver to trust the author. It gives the receiver something to inspect.

The project sits between education, research infrastructure, accessibility work, and AI-mediated knowledge exchange. Its premise is modest but demanding: if knowledge is going to move through people, platforms, and machines, the conditions for interpreting it should be able to move with it.

KNOBE Protocol v1 is authored by David Kyle, University of California, Davis, and released under CC BY 4.0. The protocol stands or falls on its specification, test vectors, reference verifier, and independent implementations. Institutional affiliation is provided for identification; KNOBE is an open protocol, not an official UC Davis standard.

Governance

Until a formal advisory board is constituted, David Kyle is the sole steward of the KNOBE protocol. That is a transitional condition, not a design goal. Trust in an open protocol should not rest on who the steward is. It should rest on the constraints the steward accepts in public.

The protocol is open under CC BY 4.0 and will stay that way. It will not be enclosed, gated, or quietly converted into something people must pay to read or verify.

Verification will always be possible with no server, no account, and no permission from anyone. The reference verifier is a single standard-library Python file with no external dependencies. That is a design constraint, not a feature that can be traded away.

The protocol will not be represented as doing more than it does. Integrity is not truth. KNOBE does not open the AI black box.

Where commercial tooling is built on top of the protocol, the boundary between the open layer and the application layer will be kept visible. The open protocol will never depend on any commercial implementation.

This comes from a research program, not a growth target. The measure of success is whether KNOBE stays useful and stays honest.

v1 is frozen

The canonical hash rule, marker format, required fields, three-layer structure, and verification semantics will not change in v1.x. Backward-compatible additions may be issued as v1.x updates. Breaking changes require a v2.0 declaration. Files sealed under v1.0 remain valid under any v1.x verifier.

Semantics follow the sealed version

A verifier must apply the verification semantics of the spec version a file was sealed under. A future verifier may report additional advisory information, but must not reinterpret a previously valid KNOBE as failed.

How the spec changes

Proposed changes, bug reports, and implementation questions are submitted through the public source repository. The steward reviews proposals, maintains a public comment period for substantive changes, and issues decisions with documented rationale.

Team and advisory board

KNOBE is being developed in collaboration with colleagues and partners working across research, accessibility, software development, and education technology. An advisory board is forming. We are looking for people with experience in independent protocol implementation, educational technology, accessibility, open infrastructure, and institutional knowledge governance.

Write to djkyle@ucdavis.edu with a brief note on your perspective and relevant work. There is no deadline; this is an ongoing invitation.

Institutional pilots

KNOBE instructional pilots are being developed for large-scale university courses, including a 500-student course operating under strict privacy and accessibility requirements.

Application-layer work is also exploring accessible credentialing, hardware authentication, and distributed-ledger options for institutional deployment. These are possible implementations built around KNOBE; they are not requirements of the open protocol.
