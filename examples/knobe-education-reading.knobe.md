# Knowledge Institutions in the Age of Context Loss

*Assigned reading, SOC 150 — Knowledge in the AI Era*

## Abstract

This essay argues that knowledge institutions — libraries, universities, journals — were built to solve a portability problem that no longer matches the problem we actually face. The original problem was scarcity: too few copies, too little access. The new problem is context loss: abundant copies, stripped of the conditions that made them interpretable. A summary circulates without the consent terms that governed the original. A translation travels without the source it was translated from. An AI-assisted synthesis spreads without the fidelity limits that defined what it could and could not be trusted for. Institutions that solved scarcity are poorly designed for this problem. The essay proposes that the unit of portability must shift from the platform to the artifact itself.

## Main argument

The essay proceeds in three sections. Section 1 diagnoses the context-loss problem through examples drawn from journalism, scholarship, and everyday AI use. Section 2 traces why existing institutional responses — metadata standards, platform controls, disclosure requirements — address symptoms rather than structure. Section 3 proposes that interpretive conditions should be sealed into the knowledge object at the moment of its creation, so that whatever system receives it next inherits not only the content but the conditions under which it was made and authorized.
