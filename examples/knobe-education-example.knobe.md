# AI-Assisted Reflection: Reading Summary

*SOC 150 — Knowledge in the AI Era*

## What I was asked to do

Summarize the key argument of the assigned reading and reflect on one place where I disagreed or wanted to push back.

## My summary

The reading argues that knowledge institutions — libraries, universities, journals — were built to solve a portability problem that no longer matches the shape of the problem we actually have. The old problem was scarcity: too few copies, too little access. The new problem is context loss: abundant copies, stripped of the conditions that made them interpretable.

I found that argument mostly convincing, though I think it understates how much institutional systems still matter for access in under-resourced contexts.

## Where I pushed back

The reading treats "the fragment surviving transit" as straightforwardly bad. But sometimes a fragment traveling without its original context is what allows it to reach a new audience who can make something useful of it. Decontextualization can be generative, not only destructive. I'm not sure the protocol framing accounts for that.

## How I used AI in this reflection

I used an AI assistant to check whether my pushback had a name in the literature. It suggested "productive decontextualization" and pointed me toward translation studies. I kept the idea but wrote the argument myself. The AI did not draft any of the text above.

## What this reflection can and cannot be trusted for

This is a first-draft course reflection, written to think through an argument, not to make a scholarly claim. The summary is my paraphrase of the reading, not a quotation. The pushback is a genuine position I hold, not a researched counterargument.
